DB RetirePlan

Calculation Methodology & Assumptions

Overview

Standard "FIRE" (Financial Independence, Retire Early) calculators operate on a single assumption: your investment portfolio must cover your expenses forever. However, for individuals with a Defined Benefit (DB) pension plan—such as federal public servants—retirement often consists of multiple distinct financial phases.

This calculator uses month-by-month compounding to determine exactly when your investment portfolio reaches the critical mass needed to bridge the gap to your pension, and subsequently sustain you for the rest of your life.

The Four Phases of Early Retirement

1. The Accumulation Phase

This is the period from your current age until your retirement date. During this phase, you are actively working and saving.

Portfolio = Previous Portfolio × (1 + Monthly ROI) + (Annual Savings ÷ 12)

2. The Gap Phase (Early Retirement to Pension Start)

If you retire before your pension begins, you enter the "Gap Phase". During this time, you have no employment income and no pension income. Your portfolio must cover 100% of your expenses.

Portfolio = Previous Portfolio × (1 + Monthly ROI) - (Annual Expenses ÷ 12)

3. The Bridge Phase (Pension Start to Age 65)

(Only applicable in MyGCPension Mode)
When your pension begins, your reliance on your portfolio drops significantly. If you are under 65, the public service pension typically pays a "Lifetime Pension" plus a temporary "Bridge Benefit". Your portfolio now only needs to cover the shortfall between your total expenses and your total pension income.

Shortfall = Math.max(0, Expenses - Lifetime Pension - Bridge Benefit)
Portfolio = Previous Portfolio × (1 + Monthly ROI) - (Shortfall ÷ 12)

4. The Lifetime Phase (Age 65+)

At age 65, the Bridge Benefit ceases (as this is when unreduced CPP is assumed to begin). Your pension income drops to the "Lifetime Pension" amount. Your portfolio withdrawals must increase slightly to cover the lost bridge benefit.

Shortfall = Math.max(0, Expenses - Lifetime Pension)
Target Portfolio Required at Age 65 = Shortfall ÷ Safe Withdrawal Rate

Key Assumptions

  • Inflation & Real Returns: This calculator operates using "real returns". We assume the Annual Return input is already adjusted for inflation. By ignoring inflation, we keep all financial figures in "today's dollars" for easier comprehension.
  • Safe Withdrawal Rate (SWR): Used to calculate the terminal target portfolio. If you need $20,000 a year from your portfolio post-65, and your SWR is 4%, the calculator ensures your portfolio reaches $500,000 ($20k / 0.04) by age 65.
  • Bridge Benefit End Age: The calculator strictly assumes the Bridge Benefit terminates precisely at age 65, aligning with standard Canadian public service pension plan structures.
  • Compounding Frequency: Growth and withdrawals are simulated on a monthly basis to provide an exact "months to retirement" figure.

Statistics Canada Demographic Benchmarks

To help users ground their retirement projections in real-world context, this calculator incorporates official socio-economic benchmarks from Statistics Canada. Because spending and income are collected through different national surveys, their data structures differ. We present these comparisons under the following framework:

1. Income Benchmarks

Income data is sourced from the Canadian Income Survey (CIS, 2024), specifically Table 11-10-0190-01 (formerly CANSIM 206-0011).

  • Unit of Analysis: "Economic Families" (two or more related people living in the same dwelling) and "Unattached Individuals" (persons not in an economic family).
  • Concept: We use Median After-Tax Income. This is the exact middle point of disposable income (all income minus personal income taxes). Using after-tax median income ensures CPP, EI, and general taxes are already factored out, aligning with the "post-tax" income input of the calculator.
  • Data Granularity: This dataset is fully cross-tabulated. When you select a Province and a Household Composition, the calculator displays the exact, official median after-tax income for that specific combination (e.g., Ontario Couple with children = $135,200).

2. Expense Benchmarks

Expenditure data is sourced from the Survey of Household Spending (SHS, 2023).

  • Concept: We use Total Current Consumption. This metric represents expenditures on goods and services (food, shelter, utilities, transportation, healthcare, etc.). It strictly excludes personal income taxes, pension contributions, and insurance premiums, ensuring it aligns with the "Annual Expenses" input of the calculator without double-counting savings.
  • Data Granularity and Limitations: Unlike income, Statistics Canada does not publish spending data cross-tabulated by province, age, and household type together. Instead, these dimensions are published in separate tables:
    • Household Type (Table 11-10-0244-01): Canada-wide average spending broken down by household composition. Because family structure is the primary driver of spending, this is used as the primary benchmark.
    • Geography (Table 11-10-0222-01): Average spending by province. This provides geographic context (e.g., higher average costs in Alberta/Ontario versus Atlantic Canada).
    • Age (Table 11-10-0227-01): Average Canada-wide spending by the age of the reference person. This shows how spending naturally contracts or expands at different life stages.
